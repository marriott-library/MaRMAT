Getting started

MaRMAT helps you flag harmful, outdated, or otherwise problematic language in tabular metadata using pre-curated and custom lexicons. Follow these steps to run an assessment.

The workflow

  1. Load metadata — Upload a CSV, TSV, or EAD XML file. Each row should be one record, and one column should be a unique identifier (e.g. a Record ID).
  2. Choose a lexicon — Use a bundled lexicon or upload your own CSV with term and category columns.
  3. Configure — Pick your ID column, the metadata fields to scan, and the lexicon categories to check.
  4. Run & review — Perform the analysis, browse the results table, and download them as CSV or TSV.
  5. Statistics — Explore an interactive dashboard summarizing your results.

The lexicons

Lexicons are carefully curated lists of terms grouped by category. MaRMAT ships with three pre-curated lexicons, and you can supply your own.

  • Reparative Metadata Lexicon — potentially harmful terminology organized by category (e.g. ability, gender, LGBTQ, mental health, race, slavery). Best suited for uncontrolled fields such as title and description.
    Warning: this lexicon contains extremely offensive terminology, including slurs and pejoratives, so that robust assessment is possible.
  • LCSH Lexicon — selected changed and canceled Library of Congress Subject Headings. Best for fields using LCSH as a controlled vocabulary (e.g. subject).
  • Sensitive Content Lexicon — terms that may indicate sensitive content (deceased people, nudity, graphic/violent content, and material that may be subject to NAGPRA). Use directionally and follow your organization's policies.

Browse the full lexicon CSVs on the MaRMAT website.

Tips

  • Metadata files should be CSV or TSV; lexicon files should be CSV.
  • The lexicon file needs term and category columns.
  • Include an identifier column so results can be reconciled with your original metadata.
  • For large metadata sets, select a subset of categories to improve processing speed.
  • Matching is whole-word and case-insensitive (e.g. "potato" will not match "potatoes").
